Chokepoint Dependency Ratio — Definition

CDR = Total chokepoint trade ÷ Total trade value
Measures the proportion of French trade that transits through strategic maritime chokepoints. A high CDR means that a single geographic point of failure can disrupt a significant share of national trade. Based on: Ang, C. (2021) + UN Comtrade bilateral flow data.

🎯 Strategic Conclusion

This scenario directly validates our Capacity Utilization KPI: Dunkerque at 85.9% and Marseille at 78% already provide almost no buffer. A Strait of Hormuz disruption at 60% would push both into crisis. France's bipolar energy gateway structure — North Sea + Mediterranean — is efficient under normal conditions but lacks resilience under systemic global shocks. The strategic recommendation is clear: develop Atlantic energy gateway capacity and increase LNG strategic storage.

RECENT REAL-WORLD DISRUPTION · 2023–2024
Red Sea / Suez Crisis — Rerouting Impact

Starting late 2023, Houthi attacks in the Red Sea forced major shipping companies (Maersk, MSC, CMA CGM) to reroute via the Cape of Good Hope. This real disruption already impacted France directly: Asia-France volumes dropped 50%, freight costs surged, and Spain gained a 13% maritime cost advantage over French ports.

EMERGING SYSTEMIC RISK
Major Cyberattack on French Port Community System

44% of maritime professionals have faced a cyberattack. The NotPetya attack caused $10B+ in losses to Maersk. A similar attack on French PCS (Port Community System) would freeze booking, gate, documentation and tracking simultaneously. LNG terminal operations are particularly vulnerable as they rely heavily on digital control systems.

Immediate impacts
  • Terminal operations halted 24–72h
  • LNG unloading suspended
  • Cold-chain agri imports at risk
  • Manual fallback = 30% capacity
Cascading effects
  • Ships queue → saturation within 48h
  • National energy buffer shrinks
  • Agrifood spoilage risk (perishables)
  • Supply disruption for downstream industry

CLIMATE RISK · INCREASING FREQUENCY
Extreme Coastal Flooding — Key Port Closure

French ports were designed for past climate conditions and face rising exposure to sea-level-driven flooding. A storm surge damaging access roads, yards, or critical infrastructure at Marseille or Dunkerque would directly affect energy unloading. Extreme heat threatens cold-chain integrity for agri-food imports.

📐 Capacity Utilization — Methodology Note

Since official design capacity data for French ports is not publicly available in harmonized form, we use a maximum historical throughput proxy: the highest annual volume recorded per port per sector between 2000 and 2024 (SDES data).

Formula: Capacity Utilization (%) = Throughput 2024 / Max Historical Throughput (2000–2024) × 100

This is consistent with academic port planning literature (MAT/HEAT framework — Maximum Attainable Throughput / Highest Efficient Attainable Throughput). It reflects what the port has already demonstrated it can handle, making it a realistic and defensible benchmark.

Data Quality: Throughput data = High (official SDES). Capacity proxy = Medium (empirical maximum). All limitations are clearly flagged.

Strategic Implications

Recommendations

Based on our KPI analysis, what should policymakers and port authorities do to improve French maritime supply chain resilience?

1
Develop Atlantic Energy Gateway Capacity
France's bipolar energy structure (Dunkerque North + Marseille South) leaves no buffer under disruption. Building or expanding LNG terminal capacity on the Atlantic façade (Nantes Saint-Nazaire, La Rochelle) would provide a third gateway, reducing concentration risk and improving shock absorption. CRE data confirms all current terminals are fully booked — new capacity is urgently needed.
Energy
2
Increase Strategic LNG Storage Capacity
France's LNG terminals are 100% contracted, leaving no spot capacity for emergency imports during a Hormuz or Suez disruption. Expanding underground gas storage and LNG buffer tanks would allow the system to absorb 2–4 week supply shocks without triggering immediate port congestion.
Energy
3
Strengthen HAROPA Cereal Export Redundancy
HAROPA (Rouen) handles ~43% of French maritime cereal exports and runs at 78.8% capacity utilization — close to the sensitive zone. Developing secondary cereal export capacity at La Rochelle and Dunkerque would reduce single-hub dependency. The 2024 harvest shock (−40% volumes) exposed how close to the limit the system operates.
Agrifood
4
Diversify Energy Import Partners Beyond US LNG
France's energy imports are heavily concentrated on the USA (~78% of LNG imports). While this reduced dependency on Russian gas post-2022, it creates a new single-source vulnerability. Actively diversifying toward Norway, Qatar, and Africa reduces CDR (Chokepoint Dependency Ratio) and strengthens supply security.
Energy
5
Implement Real-Time Capacity Monitoring Dashboard
This tool. Port authorities currently lack a unified, real-time system to track capacity utilization across sectors. A live KPI dashboard — updated quarterly with SDES data — would enable early-warning detection, proactive investment decisions, and coordinated crisis response. This dashboard is the operational prototype of that tool.
Both Sectors
6
Invest in Port Digitalization & Cybersecurity
44% of maritime professionals have faced cyber threats. A PCS attack at any major French port would immediately push capacity utilization beyond 100% (as manual operations run at ~30% throughput). Investing in resilient, redundant digital systems and cyber incident response plans is essential infrastructure protection.
Both Sectors
🎓 Mission Value — Why This Dashboard Matters

This dashboard translates academic KPIs into an operational decision-support tool for public port authorities and policymakers. It answers the core question of our mission: "Can French maritime ports physically absorb demand — and what happens when they can't?"

By combining SDES throughput data, UN Comtrade bilateral flows, CRE regulatory data, and academic vulnerability literature, we provide a unified resilience picture that no single existing source offers. The what-if scenarios make abstract geopolitical risks concrete and quantifiable.
